5 MUTCD Warrant Mistakes to Avoid

Common Errors Traffic Engineers Make in Signal Warrant Studies

Provided by CRASH LENS - Professional Crash Analysis Tools

Signal warrant studies are critical for determining whether traffic signal installation is justified. The Manual on Uniform Traffic Control Devices (MUTCD) provides eight warrants, but many engineers make common mistakes that can lead to incorrect conclusions. This guide identifies the five most frequent errors and provides guidance to avoid them.

1

Using Peak Hour Data for Eight-Hour Warrants

The Problem

One of the most common mistakes is applying peak-hour traffic volumes to warrants that require eight hours of data. Warrant 1 (Eight-Hour Vehicular Volume), Warrant 2 (Four-Hour Vehicular Volume), and several others have specific hourly requirements that cannot be satisfied with estimated or extrapolated peak-hour data.

Common Error: "We collected AM and PM peak hour counts and multiplied by factors to estimate the other hours."

Why This Matters

Traffic patterns vary significantly throughout the day. A location that appears to meet warrant thresholds during peak hours may not sustain those volumes for the required eight hours. Using factored data can result in:

  • Installing signals at locations that don't truly need them
  • Legal liability if crashes occur at unjustified signals
  • Increased delays at locations that would operate better unsignalized
Correct Approach: Collect actual traffic counts for the full periods required by each warrant. For Warrant 1, you need 8 hours of data. For Warrant 2, you need 4 hours. Use automatic traffic recorders or multiple manual counting periods to obtain complete data.
Pro Tip: Consider using video recording with post-processing to capture full-day volumes more cost-effectively than manual counts.
2

Misapplying the 70% Volume Reduction

The Problem

The MUTCD allows a 70% reduction in volume thresholds under specific conditions, but this reduction is frequently misapplied. Engineers either apply it when conditions don't qualify, or fail to verify that the minor street actually meets the criteria.

When 70% Reduction Applies

The reduced thresholds may be used ONLY when:

Condition Requirement
Speed Limit Major street posted speed > 40 mph OR 85th percentile > 40 mph
Population Community population < 10,000
Common Errors:
  • Applying reduction without verifying posted speed limits
  • Using reduction in areas with population > 10,000
  • Applying reduction when major road speed is 35 mph
  • Using 85th percentile speed without an actual speed study
Correct Approach: Document the qualifying condition in your warrant study. Include the posted speed limit sign or speed study results, and reference the community population from the latest census data.
Example Documentation:
"The 70% volume reduction is applied per MUTCD Section 4C.01 Condition A. The major street (US Route 15) has a posted speed limit of 45 mph, as documented in the attached photograph of the speed limit sign at milepost 12.4."
3

Incorrect Crash Warrant Application (Warrant 7)

The Problem

Warrant 7 (Crash Experience) requires meeting all three of its conditions, but engineers often cite total crash counts without verifying whether crashes are of types correctable by a traffic signal.

Warrant 7 Requirements (ALL must be met)

Condition Requirement
A. Adequate trial Less restrictive alternatives tried for at least 12 months
B. Crash frequency 5+ crashes of correctable types in 12-month period
C. Volume threshold Warrant 1 or 2 volume thresholds met at 80% level
Common Errors:
  • Including rear-end crashes (typically not correctable by signals)
  • Including animal strikes or single-vehicle crashes
  • Not documenting the "adequate trial" of alternatives
  • Forgetting the 80% volume requirement in Condition C

Crash Types Correctable by Traffic Signals

  • Right-angle crashes
  • Left-turn opposing through crashes
  • Pedestrian crashes at the intersection approach

Crash Types NOT Correctable by Traffic Signals

  • Rear-end crashes (may actually increase with signals)
  • Sideswipe same-direction crashes
  • Single-vehicle crashes
  • Crashes occurring away from the intersection
Correct Approach: Review each crash report individually to determine crash type and whether a traffic signal could have prevented it. Document your analysis with a table showing each crash, its type, and whether it's signal-correctable.
4

Ignoring the "SHALL" vs "SHOULD" Language

The Problem

The MUTCD uses specific language to indicate mandatory requirements versus recommendations. Many engineers treat "SHOULD" statements as requirements or ignore "SHALL" statements entirely. Understanding this distinction is critical for compliance and liability.

MUTCD Language Meanings

Term Meaning Compliance
SHALL Mandatory requirement Required
SHOULD Advisory/recommended Recommended but not required
MAY Permissive/optional Allowed if conditions met
Common Errors:
  • Installing signals without meeting any warrant (violates "SHALL" requirement)
  • Treating "SHOULD perform engineering study" as optional
  • Ignoring required engineering judgment documentation

Critical "SHALL" Requirements

These are mandatory and cannot be waived:

  • An engineering study of traffic conditions SHALL be performed
  • At least one warrant SHALL be met before installation
  • Signal not justified based solely on warrant satisfaction (SHALL use engineering judgment)
  • Pedestrian signal heads SHALL be provided when pedestrian signals are installed
Correct Approach: When reviewing MUTCD requirements, highlight all "SHALL" statements and ensure your study addresses each one. Document engineering judgment for "SHOULD" provisions that are not followed.
5

Failing to Consider Signal System Impacts

The Problem

Warrant studies often focus narrowly on the subject intersection without considering the broader signal system context. Installing a new signal can create coordination challenges, increase system-wide delay, and generate new safety issues at adjacent intersections.

Common Errors:
  • Not evaluating adjacent signal spacing
  • Ignoring coordination requirements with nearby signals
  • Failing to assess queue spillback potential
  • Not considering progressive signal timing impacts

System Considerations

Factor Minimum Consideration
Signal Spacing Generally 1/4 mile minimum for coordination; closer signals may require special timing
Coordination Will the new signal fit into existing timing plans?
Queue Storage Is there adequate storage between signals?
Turn Lane Warrants Are auxiliary lanes needed to accommodate signal operations?
Correct Approach: Include a system-level analysis in your warrant study. Document adjacent signals, their cycle lengths, and how the proposed signal would integrate. Consider alternatives like roundabouts that may provide better corridor operations.
Pro Tip: Use simulation software (Synchro, VISSIM) to model the proposed signal within the corridor and compare system performance with and without the new signal.

Summary Checklist

Before submitting your signal warrant study, verify:

  • Actual traffic counts collected for required time periods (not factored from peak hours)
  • 70% reduction applied only when qualifying conditions are documented
  • Crash warrant includes only signal-correctable crash types
  • All "SHALL" requirements are addressed in the study
  • System-level impacts have been evaluated
  • Engineering judgment is documented for the final recommendation
  • Data collection methods and dates are documented
  • Source data is retained for future reference
